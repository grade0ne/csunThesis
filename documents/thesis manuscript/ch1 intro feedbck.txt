Paragraph 1 — mostly strong, one logical gap
The four factors listed at the end (selection strength, heritability, generation time, standing genetic variation) are presented as a list but never ranked or connected to each other. This matters because your study only addresses one of them — standing genetic variation — and you never explain why that one deserves the spotlight over the others. A sentence justifying that prioritization would tighten the argument considerably.

Paragraph 2 — the weakest theoretical paragraph
The claim that ecologists have historically focused on trait means over variation still has no citation. This is an indictment of a whole field and it's unsupported. A reviewer will notice. Beyond that, the paragraph largely restates what paragraph 1 already established — that variation matters for adaptation — without adding much new. The one genuinely new idea, that standing genetic variation affects species interactions, appears in a single clause and then disappears entirely. Either develop it or cut it, because as written it reads like a detour.

Paragraph 3 — good idea, underdeveloped execution
The most intellectually interesting claim in this paragraph — that individuals differ not just in trait values but in their response to temperature — is introduced and then immediately dropped. This is actually the conceptual core of your entire study design, since you're measuring reaction norms across clones. It deserves at least two sentences of development. As written, a reader could easily miss its significance entirely.

Paragraph 4 — improved but still has a problem
The revised version is cleaner, but the Messier and Siefert citations are doing work that isn't quite right for your system. Both papers are about plant functional traits partitioned across community-level scales — they don't speak to thermal traits, animals, or adaptive potential. A sharper version of this paragraph would cite empirical examples closer to your actual question: studies that have partitioned variance in thermal performance traits specifically, ideally in animals or microbes. Baumann and Conover (2011) is already in your next sentence and is a much better fit — you might consider whether it could do double duty.

The study system paragraphs (5–7) — solid but passive
These paragraphs do a good job establishing the system and identifying the gap, but they read somewhat like a literature review rather than a motivated argument. The transition from "no studies have explored climate change effects on other inquiline members" to "this is surprising because S. purpurea depends on its community" is logically backwards — the dependence argument should come before the gap statement to explain why the gap is surprising. Right now the reader has to hold the gap in mind while you explain why it matters, rather than understanding why it matters before you reveal the gap.

The H. rosa paragraphs (7–8) — abrupt
The pivot to H. rosa in paragraph 7 is too fast. You go from the general community to this specific rotifer in one sentence, and the justification — that it's a major nutrient contributor — is entirely about its value to the plant, not about why it's the right organism to study adaptive potential. You need a sentence or two explaining why H. rosa is a tractable study organism for the question you're asking: its clonal reproduction, short generation time, the fact that it's abundant and culturable. Some of this appears in paragraph 8 but it arrives too late and too briefly.

The final paragraph — citation issue and a hypothesis gap
You updated the temperature citation well. Two remaining issues: first, "apomictic parthenogenetic (i.e., without meiosis)" — the parenthetical isn't quite right. Apomixis and parthenogenesis both mean reproduction without fertilization, but neither strictly means "without meiosis" — some forms involve meiosis. Bdelloid rotifers specifically are notable for reproducing without meiosis (they're among the few animals that appear to have abandoned sex entirely), so say that more precisely rather than using a parenthetical that could be challenged. Second, your hypothesis only addresses the direction of the response (higher growth rates, smaller body sizes at 30°C) and the expectation of clone-level variation — but you don't state a directional hypothesis about the partitioning of that variance. Is it your hypothesis that most variance will reside among clones within populations, or among populations? That's the central question of your study and your hypothesis doesn't address it.
